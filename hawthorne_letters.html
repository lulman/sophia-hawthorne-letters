¤ Letter from Sophia Peabody Hawthorne to Her Sister, Elizabeth Palmer Peabody, 8 August 1837

In this letter, Sophia Peabody attempts to correct misunderstandings between herself and her sister Elizabeth regarding Sophia's estimation of Abigail May Alcott [Mrs. Bronson Alcott] and, more generally, the sisters' differing attitudes toward "fault-finding." Before mailing the letter to Elizabeth, Sophia gives it to her sister Mary, who adds her own perspective on the misunderstanding, asking for sympathy and peace as well as communicating her frustrations with both of her sisters.

¤ Letter from Sophia Peabody Hawthorne to Elizabeth Palmer Peabody, 11 November 1839

Sophia describes events surrounding her brother George's last illness, including a visit from Rev. Edward Thomson Taylor.

¤ Letter from Sophia Peabody Hawthorne to Elizabeth Palmer Peabody, January 8th, 1859

Sophia and family are in Rome recovering from sickness. The family plans to stay in Rome for February, March, and half of April. From there the famly will travel to Venice, northern Italian cities, Geneva, and Paris. Sophia informs Elizabeth on the status of her friends in Italy.

¤ Letter from Sophia Peabody Hawthorne to Her Sister, Elizabeth Palmer Peabody, DATE

Sophia writes about the economic hardships the family is going through, Rose's current (and Una's past) development through adolescence, and a quarrel with her sister, Mary, about some books.

8th August
My DearElizabeth,

I have just read your long letter. I cannot wait a moment before disabusing you of one entire mistake, which is your impression of my estimation of Mrs. Alcott. You never heard me express for her a want of respect or love, for from the beginning & through all I have thought her a sort of sublime phenomenon — a terrible power made up of divinest tenderness— vast humanity — infinite self sacrifice — a perfect juggernaut devotion to the Right. I have loved her dearly always. How can you think otherwise. [sic] Don't you remember at that time my sympathy with her — & afterwards my writing often to her, & ever since the affectionate way in which we have ever met. [sic] I have always felt the utmost tenderness for her. I scarcely ever saw a person who seemed to me so full of pathos — amounting to the fearful. I am at a loss to imagine how you have such an idea in your mind. Does it not show what complete, misunderstandings may exist — I never thought her deformed, never moved by earthly passion.

I had a long talk with Mary the other evening about this discussing of people's faults. She said she never before understood what I meant, & she did not believe you did. It is not that I do not see people do wrong — & that therefore I necessarily am unjust — but I have understood Christ to teach that if each one looked only to his own errors, perhaps there would be juster [sic] judgment & greater improvement in individuals. When he speaks plainly I prefer to understand him plainly, for I find it the most fruitful of good. It has not been a personal feeling for particular persons — for Mr. Alcott for instance, but this general principle, which would make me feel the same if a person I never knew were analysed [sic] & decided upon. I know that GOD only can know a person truly, from my own experience, & I know that immense mistakes are made in sweeping or even in what seem cool, rational judgments of others. But as the seeing mind cannot but observe I have thought that each person had better not by talking emphasize bad opinions of men —[ . . . ] arguing pronouncing & deciding upon their motives & actions, which ever must be somewhat in the dark — but let the opinion be constantly modified or changed in the mind by the evolving of circumstances & the influx of more light. Then no one would be so cruelly wronged as some are — & there would be more time & room for the interchange of thought about things which are eternal — Christ might say Woe unto you, hypocrites! Because there was no mote in his eye to take out,1 & he could see with clear vision, what was in man, but it is not so with the less exalted. I have said to myself Oh let me enjoy & speak of the good I see — & be silent of the evil I see. You & Mary have thought me morbid about hearing certain people spoken of — I always knew this was not the truth. It was not the person but the thing that I struggled against. Even when I have mutely listened, I have always had a mental reservation that perhaps it was not so — for GOD alone could see the heart — & comprehend all the grounds upon which it acted. I can see good in being openeyed & not acting with a rash confidence — but I suspect I shall live forever & not see good in dwelling upon personal faults. For my life I cannot percieve [sic] the least use in talking about them. I never hear of a great crime committed that I do not wish I had a hundred thousand arms & powers to guard the person from being utterly condemned, because no man can know the tragedy, the struggle, the woe, the misery of the being's inward life who transgressed—and it is an infinite comfort to me always to know that GOD knows all & will accordingly award with infinite tenderness— & allow for want of light. A murder which horrifies the community never seems to me so dreadful or wrong in the eye of Heaven as the animadversions of those who think they are more righteous than the murderer. Nevertheless, I can certainly suppose that to you it may seem quite otherwise about this matter, & that you do your duty in a way of proceeding, which with my idea—it would manifestly be wrong for me to adopt. I never thought it want of charity in you or Mary to percieve [sic] failings—but that it did more harm than good to talk about them— because I know it is impossible for an intelligent mind not to take observations. But to[ . . . ] it has been productive of more good to [ . . . ] reflect as little as possible upon the shadow & live & expand in the light & I feel more love & charity & see more every day. GOD will give the increase to both our sowings since both are sown in obedience to the conscience. I never considered myself better than you or Mary or whoever — but in some things I am very different in my habits of mind. I never compare myself wi[ . . . ] th others — I try to be as simply as possible without[ . . . ] thinking how I am— & I never feel how I am except when I fall short as I often do of the mark. I am conscious of it & feel remorse. It is only in remorse that I feel myself — when I am unjust to others — or impatient — then I am smitten as with a rod — & feel how much better others are then I — but the contrary never occupies my thoughts. If I have verged to either extreme it has been that every body was better then I, ceteris paribus. Do not suppose I extend this fault finding face to face. I think this is good & productive of great benefit — for then the two persons being all that are concerned in the matter can take care of themselves, & be improved by each other's lights. But to talk & judge of a person who is not present to have his or her dictum may cause great harm & wrong. But do not ever take for granted by inference a harsh judgment. Now both you & Mary have taken up this idea of my view of Mrs. Alcott because I had said she was violent & said tremendous things to her husband. But you should have stopped at my words — & then you would have been more just — I never said I did not respect & love her — nor implied it intentionally — I think we always come nearest right when we infer the best instead of the worst thing — I wish you would not think you know what I never express. As to Beauty I would never sacrifice one iota of goodness or truth to it as you might suppose — but Christ was even beautiful — & my aim is to do so, as well as true & good — for I think Beauty is the result of the two. But I never was aware of seeking beauty in the first place. By keeping my heart right, which [sic] is goodness, I hope to see truth, & then Beauty will flow. This is my order — not the contrary. I have scarcely taken the first step; but I feel that this is the way to go. You say it is not my tas[ . . . ] think of Mr. Alcott's failings — this I did not say but it is my taste [ . . . ] talk of any body 's failings. Don't you see why I do not like to say anything. You misunderstand me almost always — or give it with your mind a little different aspect. That saying ^ you repeat about the imagination is ^not just as I meant it but no matter. I hate to explain.


Your affectionate
Sister Sophia

Tuesday 1/2 4. Nov 19th 1839
My dearLizzie,

I suppose that Mr. Taylor 2 has told you of George's 3 perfect frame of mind — of his experience of that divine glow which was so unaccountable — but so sweet & refreshing to him — & seemed so like an intimation from GOD that it was well with him —; but he would not tell you how like a tender & strong angel he ^ himself was to us all — what a wonderful prayer he offered — which seemed to bring the host of heaven down to watch & duly ward - which brought heaven into George's chamber & carried us all as on Dove's wings into the spiritual world. It was the most fervid — the most affectionate, tender & divine uplifting of soul to the infinite father — that I ever heard. He prayed for each — for the Mother, 4 the tender Mother — who hung around her life for the Father's 5 manly heart — for the sister 6 whose duty called her away — & oh bless — our dear Mary 7 he said — let not that peaceful stream be disturbed — " he prayed for the sister present 8 — 'she with the delicate nerves —' For Aunt Pickman 9 too he petitioned heaven — The tears were at last loosened from my brain which I had kept back so long that I began to feel as if they were accumulating there — & they flowed down my face like a summer rain without convulsion. After he rose from his knees, George stretched out his arms & Mr. Taylor clasped him to his heart in a long, long embrace — Every moment ejaculating "The Lord bless you! Bless you! Bless you!" in such a thrilling tone that it seemed as if blessings must come in legions at the call — George's thin white hands were so firmly fastened round his neck that I thought he would never let the angel go — even after he had blessed him — but at last he relaxed his hold. Then Mr Taylor turned to me & took both hands. Be strong said he. It is very pleasant on the other side. Be quiet like a May morning. He came with me into my room. Oh George is a perfect lamb! A perfect lamb. He is moonlight — so clear — mild — I would not but have seen him for the world. It was worth coming just to look at him. He never looked so handsome in his life. There is no distortion there. All is right. All is right. I cannot tell you all he said now. Oh he is the most helpful — the divinest spirit — is he not? George said there could not be such a prayer conceived of. Mr. Taylor is no sham — he said. George is very much revived this afternoon. He had not a bad chill this morning — hardly any — & yesterday it was two hours long Mary came today & staid two hours I carried her your letter this afternoon & she had recieved [sic] no harm. George thinks it would not be right for you to come on Wednesday. He thinks of every thing in all relations with exactest precision. Yesterday he wandered a little several times, & this afternoon while I was rubbing his arm, he was saying something — & then said something I did not hear. What did you say I asked. It [ ?] was nothing to the purpose, he replied. I was wandering. I never saw such clearness. He looks from some high place even upon his aberrations. I can write no more now I recieved [sic] a blessed refreshment yesterday P.M. beside Mr Taylor, a long — closely written letter


Your most aff
Sophy


My love to Connie

Rome January 8th 1859
Mrs H
My Dear Elizabeth

I am really too tired to write a letter, but cannot bear to let a chance slip. Una has written a sad letter which I fear will make your heart ache. I am glad to say that the Roman fever seems to have been arrested in her —; for she has had no chills for two days. She is a lily now, her lifelong roses snatched away, but during these two days I have thought to see a dawn of roses in her cheeks and lips, though she is now suffering from influenza.

Ada's fever is past. She is well of that today, but has a very painful shoulder —; and lies in bed still. I have had no moment to write to you in all these weeks —; having been ill myself in the interval of nursing others. No Rome this winter for us so far. My journal is left in the middle of a sentence, which can never be recalled now, and I am sorry, because I had an idea of the [Faun?] at that moment.

Perhaps we shall have February and March for a little enjoyment of Rome, and half of April. Then we leave it for aye and go to Venice and thence to the cities in the north of Italy & to Geneva and Paris.

Mrs. Ward is here and I have not had a moment to go and see her. But I had a long note from her. She is very happy and content in her sunny sky-parlor in the Hotel d'Angliterre — with no cares, no anxieties, only hopes she says. She suffered terribly from the severe treatment of Dr Simpson but expects to feel benefit next summer from it. Neither have I been able to see once Mrs Browning, and only once Mr Browning who has been three times to see us. Mrs Browning does not make visits. Mary Motley I see here sometimes. I have read the Dutch Republic2 during my watches, and the book is superb, I think. Miss Cushman has been here, but I could not see her. Mr. Hawthorne saw her. No society for me this winter. Rosebud is exquisitely good generous and considerate in these times, but I can give you no Altar of Roses now. Mr. Hawthorne is quite well. Here is cause for great joy — for last winter he had the Grippe three months. Julian also is well with only slight nose cold. Rose has today gone to the Palazzo Galitzin3 to see the Westons, who delight in her. She draws with a wonderful expression. Julian copies Flaxman a merveille.

I long to answer all your letters one by one — and I long to tell you much. I have sent some letters of Una to you and Mary lately. I never got yours and Mary's letters directed to Florence. Though Mr. Browning has written about them to the Post Office they do not come. I have lost all your account of dear Dr. Wesselhoeft. I endure astonishingly through all things. Goodbye with thousand blessings [.?]


Yours ever
Sophia

[Stationer's mark].
Sunday
My Dear Elizabeth, I have

become so occupied with the unhappy Weiss, that with my necessary household cares, mending & making — I have not had one moment to answer your note. It is Sunday and all the children are at Church, and I have a few moments of my own — and out of a hundred things I want to do, I take some time for you. We could not take advantage of the free lessons of Dr. Kinner for Una, on account partly of the expense of railroad fare — while there were other reasons less imperative. I am very sorry, but * since there is not literally money enough for it — I accept the disappointment. For it is certainly a very plain duty not to spend other peoples' money for accomplishments — ^or any thing else. And as we are very closely pressed this year — and feel it important not to grind Julian too closely in college, (though he must be exceedingly economical at the best) — we must all sacrifice a great many pleasant things. If Mr Hawthorne did not write, we could by no means make our expenses come within our income, and it is a pain to him to be so hard driven in his present unenergetic condition, and he doubts if we can at any rate easily make the ends meet. The Smiths have sent for Una and Julian to go to see them this winter. But for the same reason, they cannot go. It would cost at least thirty dollars for travel from our door to theirs, and Julian would have to have a new suit of clothes — costing thirty more — before he could go. As to me, I am going without any thing new whatever and I only wish I could give up any thing more so as to gratify them. As to their father he is a perfect Diogenes or a god — about going without things. He will not allow himself the smallest expenditure. Julian has had expensive books stolen by some theives [sic] among the Sophomores — one very immense Greek Lexicon, what I should think must have cost five dollars at least. Professor Gurney says this is one of the dirtiest of their dirty tricks and that there is no redress. Julian gets along splendidly as a brave and gentle Dear Sir and stalwart knight. A gentleman told me he was looked upon as a powerful lion — and one of his names is the Immortal Hawthorne. He makes no 2d aggression, but defends the weak and no one dares to touch him — as they all know him strong — and find him valiant. He has not a particle of low, unchristian vindic tiveness in his nature — They make broad lanes for him to pass when they think he might revenge some classmate's wrong — and treat him personally with high respect and the good, gentlemanly Sophomores are his friends. But if I tell you so much, you will say I am making a fine story and praising him too much. It is a pity I cannot talk to a sister without that suspicion — is n't [sic] it? When I do not answer your notes, dear Elizabeth sometimes, it is not because I accept them, but because I disagree, and think it of no use to discuss the matter. Some things in that long ago note I agreed with, however — Rose's face expresses too much critical displeasure I very well see often. She is in precisely the mood that Una was in at her age in Rock Ferry. Una was then disgusted with every body and thing that did not suit her taste & principles and seemed to fail utterly in St Paul's Charity. I was quite alarmed, until a certain Mrs. Allbut told me that I need not fear at all, for that when Una was eighteen she would be all right, and that this fastidiousness at her age was the most hopeful of all states. I find it true. Una is always excusing every body now, and finding good points in every one, and no soul is more full of generous, self sacrificing love. Every person I know in Concord bursts out to me about her in enthusiastic praise — not moderate but enthusiastic praise. You get all your evil impressons [sic] of her from the Alcotts probably, as no one else maligns her. Mrs Emerson, all the Emersons, the Pritchards, the MacKays, the Hoars, the all the world of Concord agree in love to her. They call her loveliest the most devoted the most generous and all sorts of pretty names. Miss Rose deserves your censure, but as she has a world of adoring love in her, I hope she will [soon?] love all her fellow creatures as she now loves some of them. I talk with her a great — deal about the propriety of feeling genial and pleased in society — and I shall continue to 3d adjure her. But I do not doubt she will be a loving and gracious person. For you do not know what a power of generosity she has — and all in the true Christ— spirit of not letting the right hand know &c. For this is one thing they were born with as I have brought out — to make no show of any thing they can do or feel . . . They are genuine & and all as true as heaven itself. Oh dear — now I am off on this eulogistic track. But really if you lived with them you would not love them little. And if you could see how Julian's innocence is becoming virtue, you would be glad as I am; and overflow sometimes. Religious principle is the only sure guard angelic, and I am sure they all have it. They are all reverent. I think a cultivated imagination and a delicate — or unsmothered conscience are the diamond shield against evil. Julian's memory scintillates with high, heroic thoughts and deeds. His father put into him, reading aloud, every noble story of literature. He has therefore kept the highest society — and has no love for low persons — no tastes that are ignoble. But I will stop this talk — whether or no. Who told the Queen of the Angels4 that I complained about the injury of the books? I did not — and I never asked any one to tell her — I never thought it owing to her carelessness — for I know she is never careless. But all the fly leaves were torn out up to the very commencement of the text in very many. It seemed as if they were torn out for waste paper. Somebody did it — but I never supposed Margaret5 allowed it or knew of it. She will not be so angry therefore with me — who sent her no reproach nor message and would have forbidden anyone to say a word to her — she will not punish me, so innocent, by never borrowing another book from me! And she too, the Queen of the Angels! Tell her then to lay off her crown, for she is not the Regina angelorum any longer. I wish folk would leave me to do my own fault — finding — my own duty, in short — and not try to do it for me 4th The reason I do not fancy Mrs Pugh is because she disenchanted herself by her artificialness and an effect of unveritable ness. At first I went upon your impression of her, and your talk of her to me. But very soon she became [oppressive?] and sultry and not genuine to me. And I declare I cannot help this. It is her fault, not mine. No reasoning no developments of her life will help it. Every person must stand or fall upon their character as evidenced by their presences. It is a law of the universe and no talk will alter it. I take a fancy to Mr. & Mrs. Turner, of whom you speak very [Sneeringly?] because they are single, simple, true, lovely and refined — These are very good reasons, and very good qualities.

I am sorry I have no drawings for your babies this week. But I have been overwhemed [sic] since the suicide of Adelaide Weis with cares for the rest of them — I have had to neglect many things — not however so imperative stationer's mark. as their needs.

I have the comfort of a perfect peace and order & intelligence in the kitchen — for which I am most thankful — for I dreaded Ann's going to California— away from me. There are other treasures, I find among the [Irish?] besides her. Indeed I knew this but feared not to become possessed of them.

I hope you are very well and that the Queen of the Angels is well this autumn. Give my love to her.


Ever yours,
Sophia Mrs. Hawthorne

About this View of the Journal

In this reading text of the Stephens letters, line breaks in the manuscripts are not reported; rather, lines wrap in the browser's window. Similarly, page breaks are not reported. Paragraph breaks follow those in the manuscript. Spelling, punctuation, capitalization, and abbreviations are reported as they appear in the manuscript. Text highlighted by the correspondents with an underscore is underscored in this view. Canceled text is suppressed. Text added between lines or in the margins is silently incorporated into the reading text. All material added by the editors is surrounded by square brackets: uncertain readings are enclosed in square brackets and followed by a question mark, and text supplied by the editor for clarity is set in italics and surrounded by square brackets. Gaps in the manuscript (e.g., from tears) are indicated by elipses enclosed by square brackets. Links to notes and external materials are colored.
