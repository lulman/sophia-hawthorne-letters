    Introduction: "A long—closely written letter"

    I recieved [sic] a blessed refreshment yesterday P.M. . . . a long — closely written letter
        — Sophia Peabody to Elizabeth Peabody, 19 Nov. 1839


    Salutation [1863]

    The Peabody sisters—Elizabeth (1804–1894), Mary (1806–1887), and Sophia (1809–1871)—were by any measure extraordinary. Elizabeth became a trailblazing educational reformer who founded schools for women and for young children and wrote prolifically throughout her life, publishing articles and books about education and American culture. Mary partnered on several educational projects and co-authored a book on education with Elizabeth, published a novel, and wrote a biography of her late husband, Horace Mann, among many other works. Sophia was an accomplished copyist/artist/illustrator and, after the death of her husband, novelist Nathaniel Hawthorne, the editor of editions of his notebooks and her own journals. The contributions of all three women individually to the social and intellectual life of American letters have been examined in many books and articles, including two biographies of Elizabeth (Baylor; Ronda), a joint biography of Horace Mann and Mary Peabody (Tharp), and a two-volume biography of Sophia (Valenti). Moreover, their combined role in nineteenth-century American culture has been the subject of two group biographies (Tharp; Marshall) and a collection of essays (Elbert, et al.).

    The group biographies reflect not only the cumulative contributions of the Peabody sisters but also the fact that their lives were close-knit. Sophia and Mary taught in schools founded by Elizabeth. The sisters lived together at various times during their adult lives—Elizabeth and Mary frequently during early adulthood, Mary and Sophia during an eighteen-month-long stay in Cuba 1833–1835, where Sophia was recovering from a prolonged illness and Mary provided support for her sister while working as a governess, a position arranged for her by Elizabeth. The sisters aided one another’s professional endeavors, in part by introducing one another to their personal and professional circles. As siblings do everywhere, they fought, sometimes bitterly. And, in an era before telephones or email, they corresponded regularly and voluminously about matters public and private. Many of the sisters’ letters and papers are preserved in archives—over 1,900 of Sophia’s letters have been catalogued (Valenti, email July 30; see the list of archives below). That abundance of material, and the long and complexly intertwined lives and accomplishments of the Peabody sisters, presents a challenge to any account of the sisters’ work, much less this edition of a small archive of five letters written by Sophia to Elizabeth over a period of three decades. As Megan Marshall recounts regarding her biography of the Peabody sisters,

    I had discovered early on that, even if I’d wanted to write about only one sister, I couldn’t have done a good job of it without reading everything written by all three. Their letters intertwined, so that reading only Sophia’s side of a correspondence, for example, would give only a partial view. (Marshall, "Epilogue" 249)

    With that caveat in mind, this brief introduction can nevertheless provide some context for the letters in this edition and note their predominant themes in the hope that, for readers new to Sophia Hawthorne’s story, the letters can serve as a jumping-off point, and, for scholars familiar with the Peabody sisters, provide new insights. For the most part, these are personal letters focused on family matters: expressing a personal grievance, sharing news of a loved one’s final illness, recounting family life abroad, defending children against criticism, and expressing grief and consoling memories upon the death of a nephew.

    1837: "You misunderstand me almost always"

    Sophia Peabody wrote the first letter in this collection in the late summer of 1837, at a time when she and her sisters were living with their parents in Salem, Massachusetts. Sophia, then aged 27, had returned to live with her parents in 1835 after her rest cure in Cuba. She had suffered from ill health—including severe migraines—since childhood, probably caused in part by mercury poisoning resulting from a common treatment for toothing pain in children. She was also addicted to opium prescribed for pain relief. Her time in Cuba did not provide lasting relief: in an 1835 letter to Elizabeth, Sophia described herself as “a bed case—a poor, miserable, maimed—nerve twisted—trembling, warmed concern” (qtd. in Marshall, The Peabody Sisters 354). Mary, aged 30 in August of 1837, had established a school for young children in Salem and begun assisting her future husband, Horace Mann, with his duties as secretary to the Massachusetts board of education. Elizabeth, aged 33, had two years earlier published Record of a School (1835), based on transcripts of Bronson Alcott’s dialogues with students at his Temple School, where Elizabeth and, for a shorter period, Mary served as Alcott’s assistants (Marshall, The Peabody Sisters 314). However, by the spring of 1837, the Temple School was “all but dissolved” (Marshall 326).

    A dispute over what Sophia takes to be her sisters’ misunderstanding of her “estimation of Mrs. Alcott” is the subject of Sophia’s letter of August 8, 1837—and the note apparently added to the letter by Mary. The Peabody sisters had known the Alcotts since 1830 (Marshall, The Peabody Sisters 294), but the sisters’ relationship with the Abbot’s—especially with Bronson Alcott’s wife, Abby—had been strained in recent years in part by disagreements over the Temple School and an incident in which Abby Alcott read some of Elizabeth’s letters in which she was critical of Alcott, and Sophia took the Alcotts’ side in the dispute (Marshall, The Peabody Sisters 320, 324). Here, however, Sophia complains about Elizabeth’s (and Mary’s) interpretation of a comment she had made about Mrs. Alcott:

    . . . you & Mary have taken up this idea of my view of Mrs. Alcott because I had said she was violent & said tremendous things to her husband. But you should have stopped at my words — & then you would have been more just — I never said I did not respect & love her — nor implied it intentionally — I think we always come nearest right when we infer the best instead of the worst thing — I wish you would not think you know what I never express.

    Sophia argues, in effect, that Elizabeth and Mary have taken description of Mrs. Alcott’s behavior as fault-finding behind someone’s back, something that Sophia maintains is not in her nature. She offers an extended explanation of her principled objection to “discussing of people's faults,” grounding her position in Matthew 7:3–5 from Christ’s Sermon on the Mount. In addition, she objects to what she takes to be her sisters’ unjustified interpretation of her “estimation” of Mrs. Alcott’s character based on her description of Mrs. Alcott’s behavior.

    As we shall see, assessments of character figure repeatedly in this small collection of letters, but in this situation, Mary (assuming it was she who added the note to Sophia’s letter signed “M”) is concerned not with settling the dispute but avoiding it for the sake of Sophia’s health and her own peace of mind. Of Sophia’s reaction to Elizabeth’s letter, Mary writes, ". . . she had a raging headache & has been on the bed & dizzy. . . . she said she felt as if her body was just where it was seven months ago." And of her own reaction to the tension, Mary—who, we should recall, spent two years helping Sophia attempt to regain health in Cuba—sounds at wit’s end: "I believe if you & she have any more cryings about each other I shall die. I have often wished you both dead, & I shall never enjoy you till I think this matter is not to be alluded to again."

    1839: "He prayed for each"

    The next letter in the collection was written over two years later, in November 1839, as Sophia’s brother George lay dying of spinal tuberculosis at their parents’ home in Salem. Another brother, Wellington, had died in the autumn of 1837 while working at a hospital in New Orleans during a yellow fever outbreak (Marshall, The Peabody Sisters 349–350). At the time, George had also been living in New Orleans in the hope that a warmer climate would ease his long-standing ill health (Marshall 331). George returned to Salem in January 1838, he and Sophia both living as invalids in their parents’ home (Marshall, The Peabody Sisters 359), and by the fall all three Peabody sisters were living in Salem and supporting George (Marshall, The Peabody Sisters 369), then in January 1839 Mary moved to Boston to teach (Marshall, The Peabody Sisters 379). In mid-November, Sophia wrote to inform Elizabeth—who was in Boston attending one of Margaret Fuller’s Conversations—that George had suffered “a dangerous bleeding” (qtd. in Marshall, The Peabody Sisters 388). The letter in this collection, recounting a visit to George’s bedside by Rev. Edward Thompson Taylor, followed on November 19. Elizabeth rushed home, and George died in her care on November 25th.

    Sophia’s letter of 19 November recounts a deathbed watch, a process all too familiar at a time when people with terminal illnesses typically died at home, surrounded by family members, who often shared the details of a loved one’s final illness in letters (“Responses to Death”). Such letters might convey the dying person’s attitude toward death, exchanges of leave-taking with loved ones, and any “last words,” all of which appear in this letter, as well as an account of the intercession of Rev. Taylor.

    1859: "Roman fever"

    Twenty years pass between the previous letter in this collection and the next. In the early days of 1859, Sophia Hawthorne, now 49, is living in Rome with her husband of 18 years, Nathaniel Hawthorne, and their three children—Una (15), Julian (13), and Rose (8). The family has lived abroad since Nathaniel Hawthorne began serving as American consul in Liverpool, England (1853–1857), followed by travels on the Continent, including an extended stay in Rome during the winter and spring of 1858–59. The Hawthornes returned to England in June of 1859 and to Concord, Massachusetts a year later.

    Elizabeth, now 54, publishes Universal History: Arranged to Illustrate Bem’s Charts in 1859 and is beginning to explore the kindergarten movement championed by German educator Friedrich Froebel, which will lead her to establish the first English-speaking kindergarten in America in 1861. Mary, 52, has been married to Horace Mann for fifteen years, with whom she has three children—Horace (14), George (13), and Benjamin (10). The Manns live in Yellow Springs, Ohio, where Horace Mann serves as president of Antioch College, and Mary mentors students and pursues writing projects, including Christianity in the Kitchen: A Physiological Cook-Book (1858) (ANB).

    This short letter dwells mostly on the scourge of infectious diseases, including “the Roman fever”—malaria. She recounts the toll on her family: Una is recovering from malaria but “is now suffering from influenza”; the children’s governess, Ada, has been gravely ill, probably with typhoid, but is recovering. Sophia has been “ill myself in the interval of nursing others.” Fortunately, Julian has only a “slight nose cold” and Nathaniel Hawthorne is “quite well,” though the previous winter he “had the Grippe [influenza] three months.” Illness keeps the Hawthornes from visiting with a circle of friends and acquaintances living in Rome, among them Robert and Elizabeth Barrett Browning, Charlotte Saunders Cushman, Anna Hazard Barker Ward, Mary Motley, and Lucia Weston. In sum, as Sophia writes, “No society for me this winter.” However frustrating that isolation might have been for Sophia, more frustrating for readers of this edition is the missed opportunity to learn more about Sophia’s well-documented role in reviewing drafts of Nathaniel Hawthorne’s The Marble Faun (1860), on which Hawthorne began working during the family’s stay in Rome: Sophia writes, “My journal is left in the middle of a sentence, which can never be recalled now, and I am sorry, because I had an idea of the Faun at that moment” (see Valenti, “Sophia Hawthorne and ‘The—What?’”).

    1863: "I take some time for you"

    Sophia Hawthorne wrote the next letter in this edition sometime in the autumn of 1863 from Wayside, the family’s home in Concord, to which the Hawthornes had returned from England three years earlier. Widowed in August, 1859, Mary and her sons had also returned to live in Concord, where she continued writing. Elizabeth, while traveling often, kept a home in Boston and stayed with Mary during holidays (Woodson 283). She continued to run the kindergarten she founded in Boston in 1861—near the end of this letter, Sophia apologizes for not having any “drawings for your babies this week” (presumably for Elizabeth’s students at the Pinkney Street school), one of the few reminders in these letters of Sophia’s artistic practice.

    Although Sophia’s letter makes no explicit mention of the Civil War, the war surely added to the burdens of “household cares, mending & making” that have delayed her response to an earlier note from Elizabeth. Indeed, many of Sophia’s letters and journals from the war years discuss the conflict in detail (Hall, “Crisis”; Woodson, “With Hawthorne in Wartime Concord”) as well as describing the effects of the war on her expanded domestic chores, which she describes as including “farmer,” “house-builder,” and repairing damaged books (Hall, “Writing”)—perhaps the discussion in this letter about a misunderstanding occasioned by some damaged books relates to her book-binding. But in this letter, Sophia focuses on more immediately personal matters: financial strains, and her children’s characters.

    One sentence in particular presages problems that would soon intensify: “If Mr Hawthorne did not write, we could by no means make our expenses come within our income, and it is a pain to him to be so hard driven in his present unenergetic condition, and he doubts if we can at any rate easily make the ends meet.” Nathaniel Hawthorne died mere months later—in May 1864—and Sophia faced financial difficulties for the remainder of her life (Stewart, “Mrs. Hawthorne’s Financial Difficulties”).

    Much of the letter concerns the Hawthorne children, apparently responding to an earlier note from Elizabeth concerning their characters. These passages suggest a long-standing pattern in the sisters’ relationship. After recounting events related to Julian’s early days at Harvard University—where he matriculated in the fall of 1863 but did not graduate, Sophia writes, “But if I tell you so much, you will say I am making a fine story and praising him too much. It is a pity I cannot talk to a sister without that suspicion — is n't it?” Sophia goes on to admit that Rose shows a character trait that Elizabeth had described in her previous note—“Some things in that long ago note I agreed with, however — Rose's face expresses too much critical displeasure.” Then she proceeds to extoll the children’s character until she seems to imagine Elizabeth’s reaction: “Oh dear — now I am off on this eulogistic track. But really if you lived with them you would not love them little. And if you could see how Julian's innocence is becoming virtue, you would be glad as I am; and overflow sometimes.” To be sure, Sophia’s disagreement with Elizabeth concerns her estimation of the Hawthorne children’s characters, not the importance of taking the measure of people’s character. She goes on to discuss their differing estimations of a “Mrs. Pugh” and “Mr. and Mrs. Turner,” concluding that “Every person must stand or fall upon their character as evidenced by their presences.”

    1868: "How I wish I could comfort Mary"

    In December 1868, when she wrote the final letter in this collection, Sophia was living in Dresden, Germany, where she hoped to find an affordable place to live—her financial situation continued to be unstable in large part because of ongoing disputes with Hawthorne’s publishers, Ticknor and Fields (Stewart, “Mrs. Hawthorne’s Financial Difficulties”). In addition, she hoped that Julian could continue his education at the technical university in Dresden. Elizabeth and Mary had published Moral Culture of Infancy and Kindergarten Guide (1863), which became a fundamental text in early childhood education, and her kindergarten, book royalties, and lecture fees enabled Peabody in 1867 to visit kindergartens in Germany run by Froebel’s former students [is this a direct quote?] (ANB). Based on her travels in Germany, she recommended Dresden to Sophia as an affordable place to live. Mary had published Life of Horace Mann in 1865, followed by five articles in a series written by “celebrated women” published in the 1868–69 issues of the Herald of Health and Journal of Physical Culture. Signing herself “Mrs. Horace Mann,” she wrote on child care, kindergarten, and women’s suffrage [direct quote from ANB?].

    Mary’s oldest son, Horace Mann, Jr. died at 24 of tuberculosis on November 11, 1868, and Elizabeth’s letter to Sophia of November 12 informing her of his death arrived in Dresden on December 3. Sophia’s response, presented here, expresses both her grief and her admiration for her nephew, who was a student of Louis Agassiz and Asa Gray at Lawrence Scientific School (later part of Harvard University) and published "Enumeration of Hawaiian Plants," in the Proceedings of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences at the age of 22 (“Horace Mann, Jr. Manuscripts"). Sophia also suggests that she long suspected Horace’s “constitution” would not allow him to live a long life: “I never believed that Horace would live to the prime of manhood in this world. I was sure he would die young, and so I am not surprised, however much I grieve. He had not robustness of constitution enough to bear such [constant?] and profound study.” Sophia’s reaction reflects the familiarity of mid-nineteenth-century Americans with death at an early age, especially from infectious diseases unchecked by modern treatments. The Peabody sisters had already lost two brothers in their 20s to disease and now were grieving for a son and nephew.

    Near the end of the letter, Sophia reluctantly—“I do not incline to write or think of any thing but Horace and Mary”—mentions earlier letters to Elizabeth regarding her ongoing attempts to settle financial disagreements with Ticknor and Fields, a dispute in which Elizabeth interceded on Sophia’s behalf (Stewart, “Mrs. Hawthorne’s Quarrel with James T. Fields”). After Nathaniel Hawthorne’s death, Sophia set to work editing his notebooks and journals for publication, resulting in Passages From the American Note-Books of Nathaniel Hawthorne (1868), published by Ticknor and Fields, followed by a volume of her own Notes in England and Italy (1869), and Passages From the English Note-Books of Nathaniel Hawthorne (1870), bringing to the public the fruits of Sophia Hawthorne’s lifelong practice of travel writing, typically recorded in her journals and letters. After reading her journal-letters sent home from Cuba, Nathaniel Hawthorne told Sophia she was “the Queen of Journalizers” (Marshall, The Peabody Sisters 362), and later in life told James Fields that “Mrs. Hawthorne altogether excels me as a writer of travel” (Valenti, “Sophia Peabody Hawthorne and ‘The—What?’” 59). Patricia Valenti argues that preparing the volumes of her husband’s journals “was not a mechanical activity but one that required Sophia to identify and extract from his writing, as she had from the paintings she copied as a young woman, the 'aura,' the 'sap,' or in her words, 'the—what?'” (62), thus combining her two lifelong artistic pursuits.

    Project Description

    "My Dear Elizabeth: Letters from Sophia Peabody Hawthorne to Elizabeth Palmer Peabody, 1837-1868" is an electronic edition of five letters written by Sophia Peabody Hawthorne (1809-1871) to her sister Elizabeth Palmer Peabody (1804-1894). The edition belongs to a series of collaboratively produced electronic editions of previously unpublished nineteenth-century American manuscripts held in the Rare Books and Manuscripts Library (RBMS) at The Ohio State University Libraries. Other editions include "Selected Entries from the Lucius Clark Smith Diaries, 30 July 1862 to 31 December 1862"; "Selected Letters from the Ivan S. Gilbert Collection of Stephens Family Travel Letters and Ephemera"; "William B. Anderson's Civil War Correspondence, 1862-1864"; "Journal of a Tour to Europe," by Samuel Sullivan Cox; and Louisa A. Doane's "Journal of Two Ocean Voyages (1852-1853)."

    Each text was initially edited in connection with an undergraduate or graduate course on electronic textual editing offered by H. Lewis Ulman, Associate Professor of English at The Ohio State University. The courses provided students with an opportunity to explore issues of textuality and mediation that arise when our cultural archives spread from page to screen and from library shelves to networked databases. Electronic textual editing served as each class's context for examining what happens—and envisioning what might happen—when artifacts in one medium are represented in another medium, especially with regard to the interpretive work of reading. Electronic textual editions also provided a contact zone that helped students reflect on what manuscript, print, and "born digital" artifacts can tell us about their unique properties and their relationships to one another.

    While the students' work focused on providing reliable textual editions, they also explored four threads inextricably woven into the work of electronic textual editing: the lives and historical milieu evoked by the texts; the history, characteristics, and preservation of physical and digital texts; the editorial processes and decisions that shape particular interpretations and presentations of texts; and the mediation of electronic delivery systems.

    These conceptual focuses, along with the time constraints of academic terms and the need to train students who had, typically, no prior experience in textual editing or the various technologies employed in electronic textual editing, strongly influenced the selection of texts in this series. Working with curators in the RBMS, Professor Ulman identified previously unpublished manuscript materials that were of potentially broad interest and that reflected a curatorial focus of the RBMS collection—for example, letters and personal diaries that connect private lives with larger historical contexts. Focusing on unique manuscript materials not, in all but one case, associated with other copies or later published versions also eliminated the need to compare multiple copies or states of the same work, perhaps held in other archives, an impractical task in the course of an academic term. Other practical matters also informed the choice of materials, including the possibility of scanning the source documents before the class so that students could work with both the original documents and high-resolutions facsimiles as they transcribed and encoded the source texts. In the case of the Hawthorne letters, we have edited all five of Sophia Hawthorne's letters in the RBMS collection.

    Thus, the editions in this series are documentary editions—interpretations and representations of single documents—designed to provide wider exposure and access to manuscript materials that might otherwise be overlooked, and to provide users with a variety of tools for studying those texts.

    Over the course of several years following the classes in which students worked on the editions, Professor Ulman revised the introductory materials to ensure stylistic consistency and proofed the transcription and encoding. He also redesigned the structure of the editions to meet the requirements of Ohio State's institutional repository, evolving accessibility standards, and feedback from peer reviewers. However, he did not undertake a complete retranscription of the source text or revision of the students' introductory essays and annotations.

    Selection of the Source Text. This edition contains all five of the letters in the Ohio State University Rare Books and Manuscripts Library's (RBMS) collection of Sophia Peabody Hawthorne's letters. We did not seek to include further correspondence between Sophia Hawthorne and Elizabeth Palmer Peabody because of the focus of the project—highlighting RBMS collections and providing students with an editing project manageable in the space of an academic term.

    The Sophia Peabody Hawthorne letters are the property of the Rare Books and Manuscripts Library of The Ohio State University Libraries. Any use of the material should acknowledge such ownership.

    Editorial Method

    Scanning. The manuscript pages were scanned as 24-bit TIFF images at 400 dots per inch (dpi), then coverted to 8-bit JPEG images at 400 dpi and full quality. All edges were overscanned by at least 1/4 inch. No color correction was used at any stage of image preparation, but a scan of a photographer's 18% grey card on the same scanner produced RGB values at the center of 91/95/107 after applying a 150% Gaussian Blur in Adobe Photoshop to average color values. Thus, a properly configured monitor should provide a reasonable approximation of colors on the manuscript pages..

    Transcription. Editing of the Hawthorne letters emphasizes diplomatic transcription: abbreviations, recoverable cancelations, punctuation, spelling, underlining, additions above and below the line or in the margins, capitalization, line breaks, and page breaks have all been transcribed and encoded, to the best of the editors' ability, as they appear in the original manuscripts (punctuation and capitalization often can be especially difficult to disambiguate). The representation of those features varies depending on the view of the letter. An explanation of the presentation in each view of the letters can be read via the "About this View" link at the top of each view's page.

    Markup and Annotation. XML encoding of the Hawthorne letters follows The Text Encoding Initiative's (TEI) P5: Guidelines for Electronic Text Encoding and Interchange, as customized for the project using the TEI's ODD ("One Document Does it All") process. This editorial introduction and the three "views" of the letters were generated from the main XML file using XSL transformations to produce HTML Web pages. Several of the appendices were encoded in HTML separately. A more detailed description of the markup can be found via the "Appendices" menu, and all source files (XML, XSL, CSS, ODD, RNC) can be viewed and downloaded via the "Source Files" menu.

    Introductory material (such as this editorial introduction) as well as annotated lists of people, places, and organizations mentioned in the letters are encoded in the TEI header of the main XML document. Other explanatory annotations are linked directly from the text. Separate appendices provide further information about the project (e.g., acknowledgements, editors' bios, and so on). All these ancillary materials can be viewed via the menus at the top of each page.

    Proofing. Students who created the original transcriptions proofread their own transcription and encoding. In preparation for peer review by NINES and preservation in The Ohio State University's Knowledge Bank, the transcriptions and encoding were once again proofread by Professor Ulman.

    Peer Review. Anonymous reviewers for NINES provided peer-reviews for the editions, in response to which they were revised and then reviewed by NINES editors before being listed in the NINES database.

    Production and Archival Location of Digital Assets. All of the files associated with this edition are accessed via, and archived in, The Ohio State University's institutional repository, the Knowledge Bank.

    Appendices

    List of People Mentioned

    Alcott, Abigail May (b. 1800; d. 1877). Wife of American educator Bronson Alcott, with whom Elizabeth Peabody ran the Temple School. Both Elizabeth and Sophia Peabody had a strained relationship with Mrs. Alcott (CEN 18: 481; ANB; Marshall 324-325).

    Alcott, Amos Bronson (b. 1799; d. 1888). Religious and educational reformer. Founded the Temple School, which he ran with Elizabeth Palmer Peabody, who documented his work in Record of a School (1835) (ANB).

    Allbutt, Mrs. Unidentified.

    Browning, Elizabeth Barrett (b. 1806; d. 1861). Poet, author of Sonnets from the Portugese (1850), wife of poet Robert Browning. The Brownings lived in Italy on and off throughout their marriage, from 1846 until Elizabeth's death in 1861. They were in Rome during the winter of 1859–1860 hoping to improve Elizabeth's health, as reflected in Sophia Hawthorne's report that "Mrs Browning does not make visits" (CEN 16:520; DNB).

    Browning, Robert (b. 1812-05-07; d. 1889-12-12). Poet, husband of poet Elizabeth Barret Browning. The Brownings lived in Italy on and off throughout their marriage, from 1846 until Elizabeth's death in 1861. They were in Rome during the winter of 1859–1860 hoping to improve Elizabeth's health, as reflected in Sophia Hawthorne's report that "Mrs Browning does not make visits" (CEN 15:87; DNB).

    Corlies, Margaret D. A friend of the Peabodys who help care for Sophia's parents in the early 1850s when they were ill and infirm, and assisted Elizabeth Peabody in 1861 with her Kindergarten in Boston (Hurst 131n2, 281n5; Woodson et al., 348; Marshall, Email; Valenti, Email).

    Cushman, Charlotte Saunders (b. 1816; d. 1876). Actress, the first of her profession to be elected to the Hall of Fame for Great Americans in 1915. Cushman maintained a home in Rome that served as a "social center for expatriate English and Americans," including the Hawthornes (CEN 16:532; ANB).

    Diogenes of Sinope (b. c. 404 BCE; d. 323 BCE). Sophia's reference to Nathaniel as "a perfect Diogenes or a god — about going without things" probably refers to a story about Diogenes living in a tub when he was unable to procure housing in Athens (Piering).

    Emersons. American author and lecturer Ralph Waldo Emerson (1803–1882) and his wife, Lydia Jackson Emerson (1802–1892) were part of the Hawthornes' social and intellectual circle in Concord (ANB).

    Flaxman, John (b. 1755; d. 1826). The principal representative of the classical tendency in English sculpture (Symmons).

    Gurney, Ephraim Whitney (b. 1829; d. 1886). Professor of ancient history and Julian Hawthorne's sponsor and mentor at Harvard University (Mitchell 295–296).

    Hawthorne, Julian (b. 1846-06-22; d. 1934-07-21). Sophia and Nathaniel Hawthorne's son. Julian matriculated at Harvard in the fall of 1863 but did not graduate. From 1869–1870, Julian Hawthorne lived in Dresden, Germany with his mother and sisters and studied engineering at Dresden's Realschule (CEN 15:33; Valenti 260, 266).

    Hawthorne, Nathaniel (b. 1804-07-04; d. 1864-05-19). American author, married to Sophia Peabody Hawthorne from 1842 until his death in 1864.

    Hawthorne, Rose (b. 1851; d. 1926). Sophia and Hawthorne's younger daughter (CEN 15:33).

    Hawthorne, Sophia Peabody (b. 1809-09-21; d. 1871-02-26). American painter and illustrator, married to author Nathaniel Hawthorne. Youngest of the Peabody sisters (Pennell; ANB).

    Hawthorne, Una (b. 1844; d. 1877). Sophia and Hawthorne's older daughter. While living in Rome with her parents and siblings during the early months of 1859, Una contracted malaria (CEN 15:33; Valenti 176).

    Hillard, George Stillman (b. 1808-09-22; d. 1879-01-21). Family friend and executor of Nathaniel Hawthorne's estate (ANB).

    Hoars. Presumably, the family of Judge and Attorney General Ebenezer Rockwood Hoar (1816–1895) and his wife Caroline, including their seven children, members of the Hawthorne's social circle in Concord (ANB; "Prichard, Hoar, and Related Family Papers, 1799-1948").

    Kinner, Dr. Unidentified.

    MacKays. An extended Boston family, apparently related to the Prichards through marriage ("Prichard, Hoar, and Related Family Papers, 1799-1948").

    Mann, Benjamin Pickman (b. 1848-04-30; d. 1926). Youngest son of Horace and Mary Peabody Mann.

    Mann, George Combe (b. 1845-12-27; d. 1921). Middle son of Horace and Mary Peabody Mann.

    Mann, Horace Jr. (b. 1844-02-24; d. 1868). Eldest son of Horace and Mary Peabody Mann. Student of Louis Agassiz and Asa Gray at Lawrence Scientific School (later part of Harvard University). Wrote "Enumeration of Hawaiian Plants," published in Proceedings of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences in 1866 ("Horace Mann, Jr. Manuscripts").

    Mann, Mary Tyler Peabody (b. 1807; d. 1887). American writer, educator, and translator. Sophia Hawthorne's middle sister. Married to American educator and reformer Horace Mann (CEN 15: 25).

    Motley, John Lothrop (b. 1814-04-15; d. 1877-05-29). American historian and diplomat. Following research in Europe for his history "The Rise of the Dutch Republic" (1855), Motley returned to Boston, where he was a member of the Hawthornes' social circle. The Motleys returned to Europe in 1858. (ANB).

    Motley, Mary Benjamin (b. 1814; d. 1877). Wife of John Lothrop Motley (CEN 18:174).

    O'Gara, Ann (b. 1833). The Hawthorne's Irish servant in Concord during the early 1860s (Woodson 353).

    Park, Cornelia "Connie" Hall (b. 1807). Connie Park was a mesmerist and long-time friend of Sophia Hawthorne who treated her for headaches in 1841 and attended the Hawthornes' wedding. The Hawthornes met Margaret Fuller at one of Connie Parks's parties in 1839 (Dall 382n44; Valenti, Vol.1, 154; McFarland 136–137).

    Parker, Rev. Theodore (b. 1810; d. 1860). Unitarian clergyman. The letter of 8 August [1837] is addressed to Elizabeth Peabody care of Reverend Parker in West Roxbury, where he had recently begun serving as pastor (ANB).

    Peabody, Dr. Nathaniel (b. 1774; d. 1855). Sophia Peabody Hawthorne's father (Turner 103).

    Peabody, Elizabeth Palmer (b. 1804-05-16; d. 1894-01-03). American educator and author. Oldest of the Sophia Peabody Hawthorne's sisters. (Shuman).

    Peabody, Elizabeth Palmer (b. 1778; d. 1853). Sophia Peabody Hawthorne's mother. (Turner 103).

    Peabody, George Francis (b. 1813; d. 1839). Sophia's brother, who suffered from spinal tuberculosis and died in Elizabeth's care on November 25, 1839 (CEN 15:360-375).

    Pickman, Sophia Palmer (Aunt Pickman) (b. 1786; d. 1862). The Peabody sisters' aunt on their mother's side (CEN 16:14).

    Prichards. The Prichards were a prominent and extended family who were part of the Hawthornes' social circle. Sophia and Nathaniel's contemporaries would have been the children of Moses Prichard (1789–1865) and Jane Hallett Prichard (1790–1860) ("Prichard, Hoar, and Related Family Papers, 1799-1948").

    Pugh, Mrs. Unidentified.

    Shepard Ada (b. 1835; d. 1874). The Hawthorne children's governess, who was gravely ill, probably with typhoid, during the family's stay in Rome early in 1859 (CEN 16:298; Valenti 177).

    Simpson, Dr. Unidentified.

    Smiths. Perhaps Anna and Putnam Smith. In her 1862 journal, Sophia Hawthorne mentions on July 25 that the Smiths are coming to Concord, and on August 1 that "Una has gone to take tea with Anna Smith at Mary's" (Woodson et al. 307, 308).

    Taylor, Edward Thompson (b. 1793; d. 1871). A Methodist preacher and minister for nearly forty years at the Seamen's Bethel, Boston, Massachusetts (CEN 15:421-22; Mott).

    Turner, Mr. and Mrs. Unidentified.

    Ward, Anna Hazard Barker (b. 1813; d. 1900). Wife of Samuel Gray Ward and member of the Peabody sisters' social circle in Boston (CEN 16:298).

    Weiss, Adelaide. Unidentified. Presumably, "the unhappy Weis[s]" to who Sophia Hawthorne refers is a member of Adelaide's family, perhaps her husband.

    Wesselhoeft, Dr. Robert (b. 1838; d. 1920-08-17). Lived in same village as Hawthorne, West Roxbury, in the summer of 1842. Homeopathic doctor who employed "the healing powers of water" (CEN 16:71; ANB).

    Westons. Presumably, Sophia refers a family of five sisters, one of whom, Lucia, lived as an invalid in Rome from 1853 until her death and was attended by one or more of her sisters. Lucia lived at Palazzo Galitzín, located in the center of Rome, just off the Tiber in Via della Scrofia #117 ("3994. Harriet Beecher Stowe to EBB," note 3).

    List of Organizations Mentioned

    Ticknor and Fields: American publisher of Nathaniel Hawthorne's books.

    List of Places Mentioned in the Letters

    California. United States (Lat/Long: 37.0000 -119.0000). In her letter written in the fall of 1863, Hawthorne writes, "I dreaded Ann's [Ann O'Gara] going to California— away from me." California was admitted to the Union in 1850, one year after the storied Gold Rush of 1849, which resulted in a wave of emigration to the state.

    Concord. United States; Massachusetts (Lat/Long: 42.4500 -71.3333). Nathaniel and Sophia Hawthorne lived in Concord, Massachusetts during three periods of their lives: as newlyweds, from 1842–1845; briefly in 1852–1853; and finally from 1860 after returning from an extended residence in Europe (McFarland).

    Dresden. Germany (Lat/Long: 51.0500 13.7500). Sophia Hawthorne arrived in Dresden on November 5, 1868, just one month before penning the letter included in this collection. Upon her sister Elizabeth's recommendation—Elizabeth had been studying German kindergartens—Sophia was hoping to find an affordable place to live with her children following Nathaniel Hawthorne's death in 1864, and an opportunity for her son Julian to pursue an engineering degree (Valenti 259–263).

    Palazzo Galitzin. Italy (Lat/Long: 41.903158, 12.475100). Hawthorne refers to Palazzo Galitzin, on the Via della Scrofa, Rome as the residence of Weston sisters.

    Rock Ferry. England (Lat/Long: 53.3667 -3.0000). The Hawthornes lived in Rock Ferry during a portion of Nathaniel Hawthorne's consulship in Liverpool (Valenti 100–101).

    Rome. Italy (Lat/Long: 41.9000 12.4833). The Hawthornes lived in Rome from January 1858 through May 1859. Sophia Hawthorne especially valued the large community of expatriate artists she interacted with regularly during the family's stay in Rome (Alba 13–14; Valenti 153–155).

    Sandwich Islands. United States (Lat/Long: -23.5000 -165.0000). The name given by James Cook in 1778 to the Hawaiian Islands. In her letter of 4 December 1868, Hawthorne refers to Horace Mann, Jr.'s biological research trip to Hawaii in 1864.

    West Roxbury. United States; Massachusetts (Lat/Long: 42.2667 -71.1500). Sophia's letter of 8 August 1837 bears a West Roxbury postal stamp and is addressed to Elizabeth Peabody care of Rev. Theodore Parker, West Roxbury.
